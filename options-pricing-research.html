Options Pricing and Risk Analysis: A Monte Carlo-Based Evaluation

  • ROLE Computer Science Undergraduate
  • METHODS Monte Carlo, Black-Scholes, Variance Reduction
  • TOOLS Python, NumPy, Matplotlib, SciPy
  • REPOSITORY View on GitHub

1. The Objective

This research presents a comprehensive evaluation of Monte Carlo simulation methods for the pricing of European Options. The primary goal was to explore and quantify the efficiency gains from various variance reduction techniques and to assess accuracy by comparing the pricing, performance, and Greeks against the analytical Black-Scholes model.

2. Methodology: Pricing Models

The project first implemented the analytical Black-Scholes-Merton model to provide a baseline price and analytical Greeks. The core of the work involved building a vectorized Monte Carlo engine based on the discretised geometric Brownian motion process to simulate asset price paths and derive the discounted expected payoffs.

Advanced Techniques: Variance Reduction

To address the high variance and slow convergence inherent in Monte Carlo methods, several variance reduction techniques were implemented and analyzed:

  • Antithetic Variates: Employs negatively correlated paths to decrease the variance of payoffs.
  • Delta Control Variates: Utilizes the delta derived from the Black-Scholes model as a control variate.
  • Gamma Control Variates: Utilizes information about the option's curvature to further reduce variance.

3. Analysis: Greeks and Model Efficiency

The study also involved calculating the Option Greeks (Delta, Gamma, Vega, etc.) using pathwise derivative estimators and finite difference methods. These were validated against the analytical Greeks from the Black-Scholes model to ensure accuracy. Efficiency was measured by comparing standard error and computation time, proving that combined variance reduction techniques are vastly superior.
